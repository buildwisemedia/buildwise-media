# How to Tell If Your Marketing Agency Is Quietly Wasting Your Money

You hired help so you could stop worrying about marketing. Now it's six months in. Money goes out the door every month. You see some activity — a new post, a tweaked ad, a report full of charts. But when you sit down at the end of a long day and ask the only question that matters, the answer is fuzzy.

*Is this actually working?*

Here's the thing most owners get wrong. They ask, "Is my agency good or bad?" That's the wrong question. There are smart, hard-working agencies out there run by good people. The real question is much simpler and much fairer:

**Does the work have the five things that make marketing accountable?**

If those five things are missing, even a great team is flying blind on your dime. Not because they're lazy. Because no one set up the system that tells the truth. Below is a short, fair checklist you can run this week. No tech skills needed.

## First, A Word About Blame

Let's clear this up early. Most marketing money isn't wasted by bad people. It's wasted by missing operations.

A talented person can write a great ad. But if no one wrote down what your numbers looked like before they started, no one can prove the ad changed anything. A team can run a busy month. But if a "lead" is never traced to a booked job, busy doesn't mean paid.

So this isn't a hunt for villains. It's a check for plumbing. You're going to look for five pieces of plumbing that should be there. When they're missing, the leak is quiet — and it's your money dripping out.

## The Five-Point Check

Run each of these. For each one, you're looking for a clear, plain answer. "We'll get that to you" is a no.

### 1. Is there a captured baseline?

A baseline is a snapshot of what was true before anyone touched anything. How many calls came in last month. How many form fills. Where your traffic came from. What you ranked for in your service area.

Ask: *"What were my numbers the week before you started?"*

If they can show you, good. You now have a starting line. Every result can be measured against it. If there's no baseline, here's the trap: any growth gets claimed, and any flat month gets explained away. You can't tell the difference, because there's nothing to compare against. No baseline means no scorekeeping.

### 2. Who owns your stuff?

Your website. Your customer list. Your ad accounts. Your reviews. Your data.

Ask: *"If we parted ways tomorrow, what do I walk away with?"*

The right answer is: everything. You own the site, the data, the accounts, the code. If the answer is fuzzy — if your list lives in someone else's tool, or your site can't leave without a fight — you don't own your growth. You're renting it. And rent goes up.

### 3. Is there real attribution?

This is the big one. Attribution means a lead can be traced from first click all the way to a booked job. Not "we got 40 leads." That number is almost meaningless on its own. The question is which of those 40 turned into real work on your calendar.

Ask: *"Show me one lead from last month — where it came from, and whether it booked."*

If they can walk you through it, you've got real attribution. If you get a report full of clicks and "impressions" but no line connecting an ad to actual revenue, you're paying for activity, not outcomes. Clicks don't pay your crew. Booked jobs do.

### 4. Is there a weekly operating check?

Marketing isn't a thing you set and forget. It's a thing you run. That means someone looks at the numbers every week — not every quarter — and makes a small adjustment.

Ask: *"What did you change last week, and why?"*

A real answer sounds like: "Calls dipped on this service page, so we adjusted the headline and shifted some ad spend." A weak answer sounds like silence, or a report that only shows up once a month after you ask twice. A monthly report is a rear-view mirror. A weekly check is hands on the wheel.

### 5. Is "qualified lead" actually defined?

A "lead" can mean anything. A spam form. A wrong number. Someone in another state. If no one wrote down what counts as a *real* lead for your business, then the lead count is just noise dressed up as good news.

Ask: *"What's our definition of a qualified lead?"*

You want something plain: in your service area, for a job you actually do, with real contact info, who wants a quote. When that's defined, you can count what matters. When it's not, you're celebrating numbers that were never going to call you back.

## What Your Score Means

Count your clear yeses.

- **Four or five.** Strong. Your marketing has a spine. Keep running the weekly check and hold the line.
- **Two or three.** Shaky. The work might be fine, but you can't prove it, and you can't steer it. That's a risk every single month.
- **Zero or one.** You're flying blind. Not necessarily because anyone's doing bad work — but because the operations that tell the truth were never built. Right now, your money and your gut are the only instruments you've got.

Notice what this checklist did *not* ask. It didn't ask if the logo is pretty. It didn't ask how many posts went up. Those are easy to show and easy to fake. The five points above are hard to fake — and they're the only things that tell you, honestly, whether your money is turning into booked work.
