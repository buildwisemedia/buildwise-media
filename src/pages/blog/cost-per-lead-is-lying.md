# Cost Per Lead Is Lying to You: The Numbers Home-Service Owners Should Watch Instead

You check the lead report. The cost per lead looks great. So why is the bank account flat?

If that gap feels familiar, you are not bad at this. You are watching the wrong number. Cost per lead is the headline most agencies hand you, and it almost never tells you where the money is. It rewards a full inbox, not a full schedule. And a full inbox you can't book is just more noise on a week that is already too loud.

Here is the truth we tell every owner we work with: a cheap lead that never books is the most expensive lead you have. It cost you money to get. It cost you time to chase. And it gave you nothing back.

## The trap hiding in "cost per lead"

Cost per lead is a volume score. It answers one question: how cheaply can we fill the top of the funnel? That is it.

But you don't deposit leads. You deposit booked jobs. The space between "a lead came in" and "a job got paid" is where your whole business lives — and cost per lead is blind to all of it.

Two sources can post the exact same cost per lead and be worth wildly different amounts. One sends you tire-kickers who ghost. The other sends you ready buyers who book that week. Same number on the report. One pays your crew. The other wastes your evening.

If your scoreboard stops at the lead, you can't tell those two apart. So you keep funding the cheap one — and quietly starve the one actually making you money.

## A plain example

Let's follow real money instead of a vanity number.

Say you run two lead sources. Both bring in leads at the same cost. On the surface, they look like twins.

**Source A** sends 100 leads. Most are price-shoppers. Many don't answer the callback. A handful book small jobs. After you follow it all the way to paid work, Source A is worth about **$300 a job** on average — when it books at all.

**Source B** also sends 100 leads. These people already trust you. They picked up. They booked. Bigger jobs, fewer headaches. Followed to paid work, Source B is worth about **$900 a job**.

Same cost per lead. Triple the money on one side.

Now imagine you only saw the cost-per-lead report. You'd treat them as equals. You might even pour *more* into Source A because the leads feel "cheaper." You'd be feeding the source that pays you the least — and you would never know, because the number you were handed never followed the lead to the job.

That is the quiet leak in most home-service marketing. Not waste you can see. Waste you were never shown.

## The four numbers that actually move cash

You can't manage what you can't trace to a closed job. So we trace it. For every client, the team wires the path from the first click or call all the way to paid work — then reports the four numbers that actually tell you where your money is.

**1. Qualified-lead share.** Of every lead that comes in, how many are real buyers in your service area for work you actually want? A source can be loud and still be mostly junk. This number cuts through the volume and shows you who's worth your time.

**2. Booked-job rate.** Of the qualified leads, how many turn into jobs on the calendar? This is where speed and follow-up live. A great lead that sits for two days is a lost lead. This number tells you if leads are turning into work — or slipping away.

**3. Revenue per source.** Not leads per source. *Dollars* per source. This is the one that exposes the trap above. Follow every source to paid work, and the cheap-looking one often turns out to be the expensive one. This is how you decide where the next dollar goes.

**4. Time-to-first-response.** How fast does someone hear back after they reach out? In home services, minutes decide the booking. The owner who answers first usually wins the job — even against a bigger name. This number is the one most owners are losing without knowing it, and it's often the fastest one to fix.

Four numbers. Together they answer the only question that matters: *where is the money actually coming from, and what should I do more of?*

## Why most owners never see them

Not because owners don't care. Because tracing a lead to a closed job is real work, and you are already doing three jobs.

It means every call and every form is tagged to the source that sent it. It means knowing which leads booked and which faded. It means tying paid work back to where it started. Most owners can't do that from the cab of a truck between jobs — and the old setup almost never did it for them. So the only number left standing was cost per lead. Not because it's the right one. Because it was the easy one.

That's the part we take off your plate.
